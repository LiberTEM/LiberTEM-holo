**v4.1 — 2026-04-20**

> Scope: medium-term synthetic research program. The unit of delivery is **evidence that a physics-informed forward model yields better magnetization reconstructions from few images**, not a finished reconstruction tool. Real-data and unknown-shape work come later and are explicitly outside this scope.

## The scientific question

MBIR already solves the easiest part of the problem: from one phase image, recover the **projected in-plane magnetization**.

The hard part is everything the image does **not** determine by itself:
- how magnetization varies through the sample thickness
- the out-of-plane component $M_z$
- shape versus magnetization-amplitude tradeoffs
- the separation between **electrostatic** (mean inner potential) and **magnetic** contributions to the measured phase

In plain terms: **different 3D objects can produce the same 2D phase image**. The data alone cannot choose between them.

The central hypothesis of this program is that **micromagnetic physics dramatically reduces the effective degrees of freedom** in the reconstruction. A free per-voxel magnetization field has $\sim 3N^3$ independent unknowns. But real magnetic states are not arbitrary: exchange coupling forces neighboring moments to align, demagnetization penalizes divergence, and anisotropy selects preferred directions. The result is that equilibrium magnetization lives on a low-dimensional manifold — a vortex state in a disc, for example, is essentially described by chirality, core polarity, and boundary conditions, not by $3N^3$ independent numbers.

By enforcing these physics constraints during reconstruction, we couple all voxels together. This should let us extract a **better-constrained 3D magnetization** from fewer tilt images — potentially even a single projection — than a purely voxel-independent approach.

A second question follows naturally: the measured phase has both an **electrostatic** and a **magnetic** contribution. Normally separating them requires flipping the sample or multiple measurement techniques. If physics pins down the magnetic contribution, the electrostatic part becomes a separable residual. We investigate whether a physics-informed model makes this separation possible from fewer measurements.

For the active synthetic program we assume the **material family is known** (we have good values for $M_s$ and $A_\text{ex}$ from literature or prior characterization). The material parameters are inputs to the physics model, not the primary unknowns.

A differentiable micromagnetic solver (NeuralMag) gives two possible benefits:

$$\theta \;\xrightarrow{\text{define}}\; E[\mathbf{m}] \;\xrightarrow{\text{LLG / relax}}\; \mathbf{m}^*(\theta) \;\xrightarrow{\text{RDFC}}\; \varphi(\theta)$$

Read this arrow left to right:
- **$\theta$**: the material parameters we choose or fit, such as $A_\text{ex}$, $M_s$, and $K_u$
- **define**: those parameters define the micromagnetic energy model
- **$E[\mathbf{m}]$**: the energy functional of a candidate magnetization field $\mathbf{m}$
- **LLG / relax**: run Landau-Lifshitz-Gilbert dynamics, or another relaxation procedure, to move the system toward an equilibrium
- **$\mathbf{m}^*(\theta)$**: the relaxed equilibrium magnetization produced by the material parameters $\theta$
- **RDFC**: pass that equilibrium magnetization through the holography forward operator
- **$\varphi(\theta)$**: the predicted phase image generated by the material parameters $\theta$

1. It can act as a **physics regularizer** when we invert for magnetization directly — coupling all voxels through exchange, demag, and anisotropy so the optimizer cannot explore unphysical 3D states.
2. It can enable **electrostatic/magnetic phase separation**: if the magnetic phase is well-constrained by physics, the electrostatic contribution (mean inner potential × thickness) becomes identifiable as the residual.

Material parameter fitting (L3) remains a possible extension when shape is known, but it is **not the headline goal**. The primary deliverable is evidence that physics-informed reconstruction yields better magnetization from few images.

This is a hypothesis-testing plan. The deliverable is evidence for three questions:
1. Does a physics prior improve 3D magnetization reconstruction beyond smoothness, especially for the **non-observable** degrees of freedom ($M_z$, depth variation)?
2. Can a physics-constrained model separate the **electrostatic** (MIP) and **magnetic** contributions to the phase, reducing the need for sample-flip or dual-technique measurements?
3. How many tilt images does a physics-informed model need to match (or beat) a free-voxel reconstruction that uses more tilts?

### The inversion hierarchy

Micromagnetics has a causal chain. MBIR inverts only the last arrow. This plan tests inverting further left.

| Level | What you invert for | Free variables | Forward model | NeuralMag role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **L1** | Magnetization $m$ | $m(z,y,x,3)$ — ~$10^5$ DOF | $m \to \text{RDFC} \to \varphi$ | Not used (MBIR baseline) |
| **L2** | Magnetization + physics critic | same $m$ | same, + $E[m]$ penalty | Evaluates energy, doesn't generate $m$ |
| **L3** | Material parameters on a known shape | $\theta = (A_\text{ex}, M_s, K_u)$ — 3 scalars, usually anchored by known material priors | $\theta \to E[m] \to \text{LLG relax} \to m^*(\theta) \to \text{RDFC} \to \varphi$ | **Essential** — IS the forward model |

L1 and L2 use NeuralMag as an **optional plug-in**. L3 uses NeuralMag as **the forward model** — it is irreplaceable because you literally cannot compute $\varphi(\theta)$ without a differentiable micromagnetic solver. But L3 is only a defensible inverse problem when the object shape/support are already known or tightly constrained; otherwise the phase confounds material parameters with geometry.

### Information budget: why physics coupling matters

A single $N \times N$ phase image contains about $2N^2$ numbers. A free 3D magnetization field contains about $3N^3$ unknowns. So direct 3D inversion is heavily underdetermined.

| Level | Formal unknowns | Effective DOF | Measurements ($N=64$) | What this means |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| L1: free $m(z,y,x,3)$ | $3N^3 \approx 800\text{k}$ | $\sim 800\text{k}$ (independent voxels) | $\sim 8\text{k}$ | massively underdetermined; any solution fitting the projection is equally valid |
| L2: physics-coupled $m$ | $3N^3$ parameters | **much fewer** (physics constrains the manifold) | $\sim 8\text{k}$ | the same data constrains the reconstruction much more tightly |
| L3: uniform $\theta$ | 3 | 3 | $\sim 8\text{k}$ | heavily overdetermined if the forward model is correct |

The effective-DOF column is the crucial insight. L2 does not reduce the number of optimizer parameters, but exchange, demag, and anisotropy make most of those parameters **functionally dependent** on their neighbors. The optimizer can no longer place arbitrary magnetization in each voxel — it must find states that are energetically self-consistent everywhere. This is what lets a physics prior extract depth, $M_z$, and structural information from fewer images than a free-voxel approach.

### Variables at each level
- **L1/L2**: $m(z, y, x, 3)$, optionally $\rho(z,y,x) \in [0,1]$ (shape), nuisance $\eta$, and optionally $V_0$ (mean inner potential) for electrostatic phase separation
- **L3**: $\theta = (A_\text{ex}, M_s, K_u)$ — 3 scalars with known material values as inputs

### Shape hierarchy (orthogonal to inversion level)
1. **Fixed shape, free magnetization/material** — proves inversion works in isolation
2. **Parametric shape** — low-dim (disc radius, thickness, taper)
3. **Density-field shape** — continuous $\rho$, bridge to FEM

### Ablation axis (the thing we are testing)
| Prior | Notation | What it is |
|---|---|---|
| None | — | Data fidelity only; each voxel independent |
| Smoothness | Tikhonov | 2D/3D exchange-like finite-difference penalty; weak nearest-neighbor coupling |
| Micromagnetic critic | NeuralMag-L2 | Exchange + demag energy as penalty via `state.resolve()`; **physics couples all voxels** |
| Full forward model | NeuralMag-L3 | LLG relax as forward model; free variable is $\theta$ not $m$ (secondary goal) |

L1 vs L2 answers "does physics coupling yield better magnetization from few images?" This is the **headline question**. L2 vs L3 is a secondary extension: conditional on known shape, is inverting for material parameters better than inverting for magnetization?

## Architecture: two modes of using NeuralMag

There are two fundamentally different ways to integrate a micromagnetic solver:

### Mode 1: Physics critic (L2) — NeuralMag couples all voxels via energy evaluation

This is the **primary mode** and the headline of this program.

```
Free variable: m(z,y,x,3)    [optionally also V_0 for electrostatic separation]

(shape ρ, magnetization m)  →  [observation operator]  →  predicted phase φ_mag
                            →  [physics backend]       →  energy penalty E[m]
(ρ, V_0)                   →  [electrostatic forward]  →  predicted phase φ_elec

Loss = ‖φ_mag + φ_elec − φ_meas‖² + λ·E[m]
```

The energy penalty $E[m]$ includes exchange, demag, and optionally anisotropy. These terms couple every voxel to its neighbors and to the global field, so the optimizer is constrained to the manifold of physically plausible states even though $m$ is formally free at every voxel.

The electrostatic phase $\varphi_\text{elec} = C_E \int V_0 \cdot \rho \, dz$ can optionally be modeled as a separate term. If $V_0$ (mean inner potential) is a free scalar, the physics-constrained magnetic phase pins down $\varphi_\text{mag}$, and the electrostatic contribution becomes identifiable as the residual.

NeuralMag is **optional** here. It adds a penalty term but the pipeline runs without it (L1 baseline).

### Mode 2: Full forward model (L3) — NeuralMag IS the forward model

This is a **secondary extension** of the program, not the headline.

```
Free variable: θ = (A_ex, Ms, Ku)  — 3 scalars (material inputs, usually known approximately)

θ  →  [NeuralMag: define E(θ)]  →  [LLG relax]  →  m*(θ)  →  [RDFC]  →  φ(θ)

Loss = ‖φ(θ) − φ_meas‖²
```

NeuralMag is **essential** here. There is no alternative forward model. The free variable is the material, not the magnetization. $m^*$ is an intermediate output, fully determined by $\theta$ (up to metastable states).

This mode is only scientifically defensible when shape/support/thickness are already known.

### Why Mode 1 (physics critic) is the headline

- **Same free variables as MBIR**, so it is a direct comparison: does physics help beyond smoothness?
- **Couples all voxels** via exchange and demag, collapsing the effective DOF from $\sim 3N^3$ to the manifold of physical states.
- **Addresses the core 3D ambiguity**: depth variation, $M_z$, and shape-amplitude tradeoffs should all be better constrained when the optimizer must satisfy energy self-consistency.
- **Enables electrostatic separation**: if $\varphi_\text{mag}$ is pinned by physics, adding $V_0$ as a free scalar lets us separate the MIP contribution without a sample flip.
- **Incremental over existing MBIR**: the backend protocol means L2 is a drop-in upgrade, not a separate pipeline.

### Why Mode 2 (L3) is a useful extension

- **Low-dimensional**: 3 scalar parameters, so the inverse problem is small once shape is fixed.
- **Self-consistent**: the predicted $m^*$ is a physical equilibrium.
- **Different scientific question**: "which material parameters are consistent with the data for a known geometry?"
- **But not the primary goal**: material parameters are assumed approximately known. L3 is useful as a sensitivity check and for situations where $K_u$ is genuinely uncertain.

### Backend interface (supports both modes)

```python
# src/libertem_holo/base/mbir/inversion/backends.py
class PhysicsBackend(Protocol):
    def prepare(self, rho: Array, m: Array) -> FieldState: ...
    def energies(self, field: FieldState) -> dict[str, Array]: ...  # empty dict = no prior

class IdentityBackend:     # L1: no physics, just passes m through
class SmoothnessBackend:   # L1+: Tikhonov-style finite-difference energy in 3D
class NeuralMagCritic:     # L2: wraps state.resolve() for energy evaluation
```

L1/L2 backends are interchangeable. L3 does **not** use the `PhysicsBackend` protocol — it changes the optimization variable from `m` to `θ`, so it uses a separate forward function (`forward_phi_of_theta` in Phase 3b) rather than a backend that evaluates energy on a given `m`.

### Verified NeuralMag inputs

This section is based on the actual NeuralMag source and demos in `vendor/neuralmag/`.

**Structural inputs**
- `mesh = nm.Mesh(n=(nx, ny, nz), dx=(dx, dy, dz))`
- `state = nm.State(mesh)`
- geometry via `state.add_domain(...)` and `state.rho`
- initial magnetization via `state.m`

**Material inputs used in the forward model**

| Parameter | Plain-language meaning | Units | Where NeuralMag uses it |
|---|---|---|---|
| `Ms` | saturation magnetization: how large the magnetization is when the material is fully magnetized; this mostly sets the overall magnetic field strength and therefore strongly affects phase amplitude | A/m | required by demag, anisotropy, external field, and field normalization |
| `A` / `A_ex` | exchange stiffness: how strongly neighboring spins prefer to align with each other; larger `A` favors smoother magnetization textures and makes sharp twists more expensive | J/m | `ExchangeField()` |
| `Ku` | uniaxial anisotropy constant: how strongly the material prefers to point along one special direction | J/m³ | `UniaxialAnisotropyField()` |
| `Ku_axis` | easy axis: the preferred direction associated with `Ku` | unit vector | `UniaxialAnisotropyField()` |
| `Db`, `Di` | DMI constants: how strongly the material prefers chiral twists or spiral/skyrmion-like rotation patterns | J/m² | optional DMI terms |
| `alpha` | Gilbert damping: how quickly the dynamics lose energy and settle down during relaxation; it changes the path to equilibrium more than the final static state | dimensionless | LLG dynamics only |
| `h_ext` | external magnetic field applied from outside the sample | A/m | `ExternalField(h_ext)` |

**How to read the main material parameters (inputs to the physics model)**
- `Ms`: sets the overall magnetic scale — known for the material family
- `A_ex`: sets how costly spatial variation in magnetization is — known for the material family
- `Ku`: sets how strongly the material prefers one axis — may be less well known
- `Ku_axis`: says which axis that is

In the mainline experiments these are **fixed inputs** to the physics model, not free variables. The point of the physics model is to use these known material properties to couple voxels and constrain the magnetization reconstruction.

**What the forward model actually is**
1. Set mesh, geometry, material parameters, and initial `m`
2. Register field terms such as `ExchangeField`, `DemagField`, `UniaxialAnisotropyField`, `ExternalField`
3. Build `LLGSolver(state)`
4. Run `llg.relax()` to get an equilibrium state, or `llg.solve(t, *args)` for a fixed-time differentiable solve
5. Pass the resulting `state.m` to the holography forward model

**Important limitation**
- `llg.relax()` is the natural equilibrium path, but it uses a convergence loop
- `llg.solve(t, *args)` is the better candidate for differentiable optimization
- Therefore, L3 gradient-based inversion is a stretch goal until the fixed-time solve is validated for this use case

### Electrostatic phase separation

In electron holography the measured phase has two contributions:

$$\varphi_\text{total}(x,y) = \underbrace{C_E \int V_0(x,y,z)\, dz}_{\varphi_\text{elec}} + \underbrace{\varphi_\text{mag}(\rho, \mathbf{m})}_{\text{from RDFC}}$$

where $V_0$ is the mean inner potential (MIP) of the sample and $C_E$ is an electron-energy-dependent constant. For a uniform material, $\varphi_\text{elec} \propto V_0 \cdot \int \rho\, dz$ — i.e. it is proportional to the projected thickness weighted by the density field.

Normally, separating $\varphi_\text{elec}$ and $\varphi_\text{mag}$ requires flipping the sample (the magnetic phase changes sign) or combining off-axis and in-line holography.

**Hypothesis**: if the physics model constrains $\varphi_\text{mag}$ tightly enough, the electrostatic contribution becomes identifiable from the residual. Concretely:

- Add $V_0$ as a **free scalar** (or a low-dimensional field for heterogeneous samples)
- The forward model becomes $\varphi_\text{pred} = \varphi_\text{elec}(\rho, V_0) + \varphi_\text{mag}(\rho, m)$
- The physics energy $E[m]$ constrains the magnetic phase, breaking the degeneracy between the two contributions

This separation is **not possible** with L1 (free voxels): without physics coupling, the optimizer can absorb any electrostatic offset into the magnetization. It is a prediction specific to L2/L3.

**Testing strategy (Phase 3 extension):**
1. Generate ground truth with both $\varphi_\text{elec}$ and $\varphi_\text{mag}$ contributions
2. Run L1 and L2 reconstructions on $\varphi_\text{total}$
3. Check whether L2 recovers both $m$ and $V_0$ while L1 cannot

## Phase 3: Regime A — Fixed shape, physics-constrained magnetization reconstruction ★ HEADLINE
*Depends on: Phase 2 (for ground truth)*

**This is the central experiment of the program.** Shape is known. Only `m` is free. The question is: does coupling all voxels through micromagnetic physics yield a better 3D magnetization than treating them independently?

This is the **existing 2D MBIR problem lifted to 3D** — and the lifting is the whole point, because it exposes what MBIR structurally cannot resolve from a single projection. The physics model's job is to fill in the information the image cannot provide: depth variation, $M_z$, and which of the infinitely many 3D states consistent with the projection is actually physical.

### Why physics coupling should help

In L1, the optimizer treats each voxel independently. It can assign any direction to any location, and the only constraint is that the z-projection matches the measured phase. This leaves $M_z$, depth variation, and many other degrees of freedom completely unconstrained.

In L2, exchange coupling says neighboring moments must be nearly parallel (smooth textures cost less energy). Demagnetization says the global field must close on itself (no free magnetic charges). Together, these constraints make most of the $3N^3$ formal unknowns **functionally dependent** — a vortex core, for instance, is forced to be narrow and centered by the interplay of exchange and demag. The optimizer cannot smear $M_z$ uniformly along $z$ because that would violate exchange; it cannot invent arbitrary depth variation because demag would penalize it.

The result: from the same single phase image, L2 should recover a significantly better 3D magnetization than L1.

### What one phase image can and cannot tell us

The RDFC forward model is a **line integral along z** of the in-plane magnetization. So one phase image directly constrains only a small part of the full 3D problem:

| Observable | Status |
|---|---|
| $\int M_x\, dz$, $\int M_y\, dz$ | directly observed |
| $M_z(z, y, x)$ | **exactly unobservable** from one image |
| Depth distribution of $M_x(z, \cdot)$ and $M_y(z, \cdot)$ at fixed $(y,x)$ | **unobservable** — only the integral is constrained |
| $\rho(z, y, x) \cdot m$ vs $\rho' \cdot m'$ with same projection | **unresolvable** — multiplicative confound |

A smoothness prior can prefer one solution over another, but it does not add new measurements. A **physics prior** does something qualitatively different: it couples all voxels through physical laws, rejecting the vast space of non-physical 3D states and effectively reducing the degrees of freedom the data must constrain.

Phase 3 asks two questions:
1. **Magnetization quality**: does micromagnetic physics yield a measurably better 3D magnetization than smoothness alone, especially for the non-observable degrees of freedom?
2. **Electrostatic separation** (extension): if we add $V_0$ as a free scalar alongside $m$, can the physics-constrained model separate the electrostatic (MIP) and magnetic contributions to the total phase?

### Setup
- Known `rho_true` (from Phase 2)
- Known `φ_true` (from Phase 2)
- Free variable: `m ∈ R^(Z,Y,X,3)` with **hard** projection to unit sphere inside `ρ > 0.5` applied inside the inner loop on every step (not as a post-hoc cleanup). Without this, A.2 will shrink $|m|$ to cheat the energy penalty and look worse than A.1 for reasons unrelated to physics.

### Three reconstructions to compare (+ electrostatic extension)
| Label | Prior | Implementation |
|---|---|---|
| A.0 | None | Data fidelity only; each voxel independent |
| A.1 | Smoothness | `SmoothnessBackend` — 3D finite-difference exchange; weak local coupling |
| A.2 | Micromagnetic | `NeuralMagBackend` — exchange + demag; **full physics coupling** |

For each: optax loop, `max_iter = 500`, same init (zero magnetization). Record loss history and final `m_recon`.

**Extension experiment — electrostatic separation (A.2e):**
| Label | Prior | Free variables | Implementation |
|---|---|---|---|
| A.2e | Micromagnetic + electrostatic | $m$ + $V_0$ (MIP scalar) | Same as A.2, but forward model predicts $\varphi_\text{mag} + C_E V_0 \int \rho\, dz$ and $V_0$ is jointly optimized |

Ground truth for A.2e: generate $\varphi_\text{total} = \varphi_\text{mag}(\rho, m_\text{true}) + \varphi_\text{elec}(\rho, V_{0,\text{true}})$ with a known MIP value. Test whether L2 recovers both $m$ and $V_0$ while L1 cannot.

### Metrics — split into directly observed vs. not directly observed

**Observable (MBIR is expected to do well on all priors):**
- `M_proj` error: `‖∫ m_recon dz − ∫ m_true dz‖ / ‖∫ m_true dz‖` for in-plane components only
- Phase residual: `‖forward_phase(rho_true, m_recon) − φ_true‖ / ‖φ_true‖`
- Convergence speed (iters to fixed data-loss threshold)

**Not directly observed (this is where NeuralMag must prove itself):**
- **Out-of-plane recovery**: per-voxel error in $M_z$: `‖m_recon[..., 2] − m_true[..., 2]‖_\text{RMSE}`
- **Depth localization**: for a fixed $(y, x)$ inside the disc, correlation between $M_x(z)$ profiles of recon and truth. Baseline is the trivial "smeared uniformly along z" solution, which has zero depth information. Report relative improvement over that baseline.
- **Vortex-core z-position**: if the test state has a vortex, locate the core $\operatorname{argmax}_z M_z(z, y_c, x_c)$ and report error. MBIR-only should be flat; physics should localize.
- **Equilibrium consistency**: residual of $\mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{H}_\text{eff}(m_\text{recon}) = 0$. This must be evaluated using **different** energy terms than those used in the A.2 penalty (e.g. include anisotropy or DMI, or use a stricter tolerance) — otherwise A.2 scores trivially well because it was optimizing exactly this quantity.

**Shape-amplitude confound (separate diagnostic):**
- Scale `rho_true` by 1.5 and `m_true` by 1/1.5 (so the product — and therefore the projected phase — is unchanged). Rerun each prior and check whether any reconstruction recovers the unscaled $|m|$. A.0/A.1 cannot: the data is invariant under this rescaling. Only the hard $|m|=1$ constraint in A.2 breaks the degeneracy.

### Interpreting the outcomes

| Pattern | Conclusion |
|---|---|
| A.0 ≈ A.1 ≈ A.2 on **observable** metrics | Expected — MBIR works in its projected subspace regardless of prior |
| A.1 ≈ A.2 on **3D ambiguity** metrics | Smoothness is enough; physics coupling does not help beyond local regularization |
| A.2 >> A.1 on **3D ambiguity** metrics | **Physics coupling is necessary** — it constrains the 3D magnetization where the image alone cannot |
| Only A.2 breaks the shape-amplitude confound | **NeuralMag's $\|m\|=1$ constraint is doing real work** |
| A.2e recovers $V_0$ within 10% while A.0/A.1 cannot | **Physics-constrained reconstruction enables electrostatic/magnetic separation** |
| A.2 ≈ A.0 everywhere | Something is wrong — physics term not wired in / λ mis-tuned |

The not-directly-observed metrics are the main test. If we only report projected in-plane error, all methods may look similar. **The headline result is whether A.2 yields a measurably better 3D magnetization from a single image than A.0/A.1.**

## Phase 3b: Material parameter sensitivity study (L3, secondary)
*Depends on: Phase 2 (ground truth) + Phase 3 (baseline comparison)*

> **This phase is secondary to Phase 3.** The primary deliverable is better magnetization from physics coupling. Phase 3b is an optional extension that tests whether the phase image also constrains material parameters when shape is known.

Instead of inverting for a full 3D magnetization field, infer **a few material scalars** and let the micromagnetic model predict the equilibrium state. The material family is assumed known, so this is a **sensitivity study** around nominal values, not a discovery problem.

**Precondition:** shape/support are known. Without known shape, this inverse is not well-posed from one phase image, because even small uncertainty in $M_s$, $A_\text{ex}$, and $K_u$ is confounded with thickness, support, and shape anisotropy.

### Concept

```
Free: θ = (A_ex, M_s, K_u)                    # 3 scalars, usually searched near nominal values
Fixed: shape ρ_true, mesh, initial m-ansatz

Forward(θ):
  1. Build NeuralMag state with material constants θ
  2. LLG relax from vortex ansatz → m*(θ)       # NeuralMag does the physics
  3. Adapter: m*(θ) → LiberTEM ordering
  4. RDFC: forward_phase(ρ_true, m*(θ)) → φ(θ)  # LiberTEM-holo does the observation

Loss = ‖φ(θ) − φ_meas‖²

First target: evaluate this loss by grid search.
Stretch target: differentiate through a fixed-time solve and use gradients.
```

### Why this is different from Phase 3

| | Phase 3 (L2) | Phase 3b (L3) |
|---|---|---|
| Free variable | $m(z,y,x,3)$ — ~$10^5$ values | $\theta = (A_\text{ex}, M_s, K_u)$ — 3 values, usually near a known-material prior |
| 3D ambiguity | Large; physics prior **regularizes** it | Reduced by the small parameterization, but not guaranteed removed |
| Output | "what does the magnetization look like?" | "for this known shape, which material is consistent with the data?" |
| NeuralMag role | Optional energy critic | **Essential** forward model |
| Equilibrium guarantee | Only if physics prior is strong enough | Yes, for the chosen model and initialization |
| Computational cost per step | One RDFC call | One LLG relaxation + one RDFC call |

### Setup
- Ground truth: a known-material disc from Phase 2, but with **nonzero $K_u$** (e.g. a permalloy-like disc with a small hard-axis $K_u \sim 10^4$ J/m$^3$ along $\hat{y}$, or a different known material with its own nominal $M_s$ and $A$). Using $K_u = 0$ as ground truth would make $K_u$-axis identifiability a tautology.
- Known shape $\rho_\text{true}$, known mesh. The NeuralMag state used for inversion **must use the same `dx = dy = dz` as the ground truth** — otherwise $A_\text{ex}$ recovery is biased by the discretization.
- Known $\varphi_\text{true}$ from Phase 2
- Nominal material prior: use literature or independently characterized values for $M_s$ and $A_\text{ex}$ as the center of the search window.
- Initial guess: start with $\pm 10$ to $20\%$ perturbations around the nominal $M_s$ and $A_\text{ex}$ values; do **not** start with order-of-magnitude scans unless you are explicitly doing a sensitivity study. Larger perturbations are likely to relax into a different magnetic state class (vortex → C-state → single-domain), producing loss-landscape cliffs rather than smooth valleys.

### Optimization approaches (in order of simplicity)

**3b-i. Grid search (first priority)**
- Sweep a **local window** around the known-material prior, for example $A_\text{ex} \in [0.8, 1.2] A_{\text{ex},0}$, $M_s \in [0.9, 1.1] M_{s,0}$, and $K_u \in [0, 5] \times 10^4$. Only widen the $A_\text{ex}$ and $M_s$ windows if the local study shows meaningful sensitivity.
- Start with a **$32^3$ mesh and a coarse $\sim 5^3 = 125$-point grid** pilot to measure wall-clock cost per LLG relax and to find the rough landscape shape. Only scale to $64^3$ and $\sim 10^3$ grid points once the pilot confirms the cost is tolerable.
- Each grid point needs one LLG relax + one RDFC. At $64^3$ a single relax is typically seconds-to-minutes on GPU; $10^3$ points is therefore hours, not minutes. Treat this as the dominant compute budget of the program.
- Plot loss landscape: $L(\theta)$ over each pair of parameters, with the nominal material point marked explicitly
- Check: is the minimum unique? Or do several parameter combinations fit equally well?
- **Metastability budget**: relax from at least 2 initial states per $\theta$ (e.g. vortex ansatz + uniform in-plane) and report the min-loss branch. Extreme $\theta$ will often switch state class, and a single-init sweep will show discontinuous cliffs that are artifacts of branch changes, not of identifiability.

**3b-ii. Gradient-based solve (deferred)**
- **Not part of the near-term program.** Prerequisite: a standalone diffrax-through-LLG toy problem (few voxels, short fixed-time solve) demonstrating stable, finite gradients end-to-end. Only once that works does gradient-based L3 become a realistic option.
- Expected failure modes when attempted: gradients through long stiff LLG relaxations can vanish, explode, or be dominated by numerical noise near equilibrium; reverse-mode memory through many diffrax steps is non-trivial at $64^3$.
- If it eventually works: optax adam or L-BFGS with 3 scalar parameters.

**3b-iii. Bayesian optimization (fallback)**
- If gradients through LLG are unreliable, use gradient-free BO
- 3 parameters — well within BO's sweet spot
- Gives uncertainty estimates on recovered $\theta$

### Metrics

- **Parameter recovery**: $|\theta_\text{recon} - \theta_\text{true}| / \theta_\text{true}$ for each of $A_\text{ex}, M_s, K_u$, interpreted relative to the known-material prior.
- **Phase residual**: $\|\varphi(\theta_\text{recon}) - \varphi_\text{true}\| / \|\varphi_\text{true}\|$
- **Magnetization quality**: compare $m^*(\theta_\text{recon})$ to $m^*(\theta_\text{true})$ using the same 3D-ambiguity metrics as Phase 3 (especially $M_z$, depth, equilibrium consistency). Here these metrics come from the physics model, not from fitting a free 3D magnetization field.
- **Identifiability**: Hessian $\nabla^2_\theta L$ at the minimum or simpler loss-landscape slices. If a direction is very flat, the data does not constrain that parameter combination well.
- **Loss landscape**: 2D slices through $(A_\text{ex}, M_s)$, $(A_\text{ex}, K_u)$, $(M_s, K_u)$ — look for valleys, ridges, and local minima around the nominal material point.

### Interpreting the outcomes

**Realistic expected outcome (stated up front so results are not oversold):**
- $M_s$ may be locally calibratable because it scales $\varphi$ globally, but once the material class is known this is mostly a **small correction** problem, not a discovery problem.
- $A_\text{ex}$ is likely **weakly** identifiable via domain-wall width and vortex-core size, producing an elongated valley in the loss landscape even when searched only locally.
- $K_u$ is still the most plausible genuinely informative material degree of freedom for one phase image, but it may remain **near-flat** along at least one direction.
- The flatness of the landscape is itself the result. A "success" is either tight recovery of $K_u$ plus sensible local calibration of $M_s$ and $A_\text{ex}$, or a clear identifiability map showing which directions should simply be fixed to prior values.

| Pattern | Conclusion |
|---|---|
| $\theta_\text{recon} \approx \theta_\text{true}$ within 5% on all three | Strong evidence that the local known-material calibration problem is identifiable in this synthetic setting |
| Only small corrections in $M_s$ are recoverable while $A_\text{ex}$ and $K_u$ are flat | Expected. Keep $A_\text{ex}$ fixed, treat $M_s$ as a nuisance calibration, and do not oversell one-image material recovery. |
| Unique minimum in loss landscape | Good sign for identifiability in this setup |
| Elongated valley along $(A_\text{ex}, K_u)$ around the nominal prior | Expected; quantifies a local identifiability limit rather than a full unknown-material ambiguity |
| Multiple local minima (e.g. vortex vs single-domain) | **Metastable states** are a real confound — need initialization control or marginalization |
| Discontinuous cliffs in the landscape | Likely branch switches, not true non-identifiability. Cross-check with the 2-init metastability sweep before interpreting. |
| Gradient through LLG fails (NaN / vanishes) | Expected if attempted before 3b-ii prerequisites are met; stay in grid search / BO |

### The metastable-state problem

Same $\theta$ can produce different equilibria depending on initial conditions (e.g. vortex vs. flower state). Two approaches:
1. **Fix initialization**: always start LLG from vortex ansatz. Valid if the experimental sample is also in a vortex state. Breaks if you don't know the magnetic state class a priori.
2. **Marginalize**: for each $\theta$, relax from $N$ initial conditions, pick the one with lowest loss. Expensive but robust. $N = 4-8$ covers the main states for a disc.

### Role of Phase 3b relative to Phase 3

Phase 3 (L2) gives us a reconstructed magnetization using physics coupling. L3 asks a complementary question: for a **known shape and known material family**, can phase data constrain the **remaining material uncertainty** ($K_u$, small corrections to $M_s$/$A_\text{ex}$)? Even a negative result is useful — it confirms that material parameters should simply be fixed at known values and the program should focus on the magnetization-quality gains from Phase 3.

## Future directions (out of current scope)

*Only start these after Phase 3 has produced its headline magnetization-quality results.*

- **Multi-tilt diagnostic**: 2–3 small tilts ($\pm 10°$) to test whether additional viewing angles measurably reduce the 3D ambiguity. Requires a tilt-aware differentiable forward model (Phase-1-scale work). This is the most natural next step to quantify the "few images" advantage of physics coupling.
- **Electrostatic separation with spatially varying MIP**: extend the A.2e experiment to heterogeneous samples where $V_0(x,y,z)$ varies spatially.
- **Parametric shape inversion**: invert for low-dimensional disc parameters (center, radius, thickness) jointly with `m`. Requires Phase 3 baseline.
- **Spatially varying material** (`L4`): move beyond uniform $\theta$ only if the uniform-material study is actually informative.
- **Free-form shape** (`ρ = sigmoid(ρ_raw)`): most ill-posed variant; bridge to FEM / topology-optimization-style work.
- **Warm-start study**: compare zero init, analytic ansatz, and LLG-relaxed random init across regimes.
- **Experimental data**: phase calibration, pixel-size matching, tilt alignment, support estimation, uncertainty weighting. Entirely separate program.

## Decisions log

| Decision | Rationale |
|---|---|
| **Phase 3 (L2) is the headline** | Physics coupling reduces effective DOF and should yield better magnetization from few images — this is the primary scientific question. |
| **Electrostatic separation via A.2e** | If physics pins down the magnetic phase, the MIP becomes identifiable as the residual — a unique benefit of the physics-informed approach. |
| Material parameters are inputs, not unknowns | Material family is known; $M_s$ and $A_\text{ex}$ are fixed inputs to the physics model. |
| Phase 3b (L3) is secondary | Material sensitivity is worth testing but is not the main deliverable. |
| L3 gradient path is deferred | Gradients through stiff LLG are unreliable until a standalone diffrax-through-LLG toy is proven. |
| Metastability sweep with ≥ 2 inits per $\theta$ | Single-init sweeps show cliffs from branch switches, not identifiability structure. |
| Ground truth uses nonzero $K_u$ | $K_u = 0$ makes $K_u$ identifiability a tautology. |
| Hard unit-norm projection in A.2 | Soft penalty lets the optimizer shrink $\|m\|$ to cheat the energy term. |
| Grid search before gradient descent for L3 | First prove the inverse problem is informative before relying on differentiable LLG. |
| `PhysicsBackend` protocol | Backend-neutral; FEM becomes a future backend. |
| Isotropic voxels, phase-only | Sound v1 scope. |
| Analytic vortex before LLG vortex | Isolate forward-model bugs from physics-solver bugs. |
